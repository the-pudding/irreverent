May Quarantine 2020

7m read

Amber Thomas

My Netflix account and I have been spending a lot of time together. Probably too much time together, honestly. Lately, I’ve noticed that if I leave the TV app idle, a screensaver cycles through titles on the platform paired with three descriptive words. Things like “charming, witty, funny”.

And over time, I’ve noticed a single, maddening word used more than the rest...

Netflix Screensaver Simulator

Irreverent. Irreverent. Irreverent.

C’mon Netflix, is this really the word you want to use? Surely there are other words out there. On my account, it was the most common tag, appearing alongside 26% of advertised titles.

If, like me, you need a little refresher on the definition of irreverent, don’t feel bad. It’s the only common tag that’s an actual SAT word. Here’s the definition:

Irreverent: showing a lack of respect for people or things that are generally taken seriously

Interestingly enough, this implies that by definition it shouldn’t be a common descriptor for media. Afterall, if everything is poking fun at things generally taken seriously, is anything taking them seriously? If everything is irreverent, is anything? Either way, I was starting to see the word irreverent in my dreams.

Now, I know what you’re going to say. Netflix’s algorithms are probably surfacing things that it thinks I would like based on my own viewing preferences. It’s certainly possible that I’ve been causing my own irreverent doom.

So, I created a brand new profile with no viewing preferences, which according to Netflix, should display “a diverse and popular set of titles”. Even with this new account, irreverent was the second most common tag.

That’s so much irreverence. What is going on here? Why is the word irreverent being used so much?

Even though it irks me personally, there may be a reason for using this specific word: it’s what certain viewers want. In a 2012 interview with the Los Angeles Times, the overseer of Netflix’s tagging program, Todd Yellin, described the team’s process of choosing the word cerebral over the more-accessible brainy. He said “...we concluded that if you like cerebral titles, then ‘cerebral’ will be a good word for you”. Since, irreverent is a real genre of comedy, maybe its heavy use throughout Netflix is to specifically target users who know of and enjoy that specific sub-genre.

That may get at why irreverent is used instead of another descriptor (Thesaurus.com suggests the synonyms “cheeky”, “flippant”, or “tongue-in-cheek”), but it doesn’t explain why so many irreverent titles are being surfaced.

For that, we may be able to look to COVID-19. Sure, when stay-at-home orders first started rippling across the US, it seemed like everyone was watching movies about pandemics. But Google searches for “comedy movies” also peaked around that same time. We can’t know for sure if users were looking for irreverent comedies, of course, but if there were ever a time to poke fun at things like our government, wealth, or the way we work… this may be it.

Even researchers agree that watching comedies during times of uncertainty can have a “positive effect”. In an interview with CBC, Dr. Shira Gabriel, an associate professor of psychology at SUNY, University at Buffalo explained that “it can be comforting to make light of the very things that worry us. It can make those things seem less scary and remind us that we are not alone.” Maybe Netflix is doing us a solid by putting these titles front and center, trying to give us a nudge to pick something light during these tough times. Or maybe they’re just letting us know that other users are rewatching The Office or Schitt’s Creek for the umpteenth time and you may want to finally give it a try.
